Salt and Salt Products

üßÇ Simple tests to detect adulterants in table salt and related products

1. Chalk Powder in Salt

Dissolve salt in water. Chalk powder settles at the bottom, while pure salt dissolves completely.

2. White Powder Adulterants

Rub salt between fingers. If it feels gritty or leaves residue, it may contain insoluble impurities.

3. Iodine Deficiency in Iodized Salt

Add starch solution and lemon juice to salt. A blue color confirms iodine presence; no color means deficiency.

4. Washing Soda in Salt

Place a drop of the solution onto a strip of red litmus paper using a glass rod. If the red litmus paper turns blue, the salt is basic, indicating the presence of washing soda.

5. Presence of Sand

Spread salt on a flat surface. Sand particles are visible and do not dissolve in water.

6. Moisture in Salt

Pure salt is dry and free-flowing. Moist salt clumps together and may indicate poor storage or contamination.

7. Bitter Taste in Salt

Bitter taste may indicate magnesium salts or chemical contamination. Avoid consumption and report if persistent.

8. Artificial Whitening Agents

Excessively white salt may contain bleaching agents. Check for unnatural brightness and chemical smell.
